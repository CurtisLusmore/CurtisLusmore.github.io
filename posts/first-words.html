First Words

Curtis Lusmore, 31 January 2023

One of the things that you do when you become a parent for the first time is you imagine what your child’s first word will be. I remember thinking that the most likely words would be mama or dada, but with so many words to choose from, the likelihood would be that it would be some other random word like book, shoe or head.

What I never imagined was how hard it would be to define "first" or "word".

To adults, words seem to be a fairly concrete concept. A simple and hopefully uncontroversial definition of "word" might be "a sound uttered intentionally with a specific meaning understood by both speaker and listener". I’m not sure if I ever thought it through this thoroughly, but I think I always imagined that the thing I considered to be my child’s first word would be reasonably fully-formed, I would be reasonably sure it was intentional, and reasonably sure that they were trying to convey meaning.

But those are the three key points that I’ll keep coming back to--a sound, intention, and meaning.

Babies start babbling quite early on and will try to mimic various aspects of what you say to them, like sound and rhythm. At this early stage, they aren’t able to reliably reproduce the same sound, they are merely responding to what they hear so they are not intentional about what sounds they make, and certainly no meaning is conveyed. Their ability to make sounds, form intentions, and understand meaning each develop as they grow.

The range of sounds they can make grows at a fairly steady pace with practice as they continue to repeat what they hear.
